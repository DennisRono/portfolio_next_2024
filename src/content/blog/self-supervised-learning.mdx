# Self-Supervised Learning

Self-supervised learning (SSL) enables AI systems to learn meaningful representations from unlabeled data by creating supervisory signals from the data itself.

## What is Self-Supervised Learning?

SSL creates learning tasks from unlabeled data—predicting masked portions, matching augmented views, or solving other pretext tasks—to learn useful representations without manual labels.

## Why Self-Supervised Learning?

### Abundant Unlabeled Data

The vast majority of available data is unlabeled; SSL unlocks its potential.

### Reduced Annotation Costs

Less reliance on expensive and time-consuming manual labeling.

### Better Representations

SSL often learns more general and transferable features than supervised learning.

## Key Approaches

### Contrastive Learning

Learning by contrasting positive pairs (similar samples) against negative pairs (different samples).

### Masked Prediction

Predicting masked or hidden portions of the input (e.g., masked language modeling in BERT).

### Clustering-Based Methods

Grouping similar samples and learning to predict cluster assignments.

### Distillation Methods

Learning from different views or augmentations without explicit negative samples.

## Notable Methods

### SimCLR and MoCo

Contrastive learning frameworks for visual representations.

### BERT and GPT

Masked language modeling and next-token prediction for language.

### DINO and DINOv2

Self-distillation approaches for vision transformers.

### MAE (Masked Autoencoders)

Masked image modeling for efficient visual representation learning.

## Applications

- Foundation models for language and vision
- Transfer learning to downstream tasks
- Learning from domain-specific unlabeled data
- Multimodal representation learning

## Challenges

- Designing effective pretext tasks
- Computational cost of training
- Evaluation and comparison of learned representations
- Understanding what representations capture
